\begin{abstract}
In his 1905 ``miraculous year'' paper on the photoelectric effect, Albert
Einstein proposed that electromagnetic radiation consists of discrete energy
quanta~$h\nu$.  His argument had two components: a physical \emph{model}
(the light-quantum hypothesis) and an implied \emph{measurement equation}
relating the observable stopping potential~$V_0$ linearly to the incident
frequency~$\nu$.  This note examines Einstein's reasoning from a modern
statistical perspective.  I assemble the historical data available in
1902--1916---Lenard's qualitative findings, the intermediate quantitative
work of Hughes (1912) and Richardson \& Compton (1912), and Millikan's
definitive measurements (1916)---and carry out the simple ordinary-least-squares
regression that the theory implies: $V_0 = \alpha + \beta\nu + \varepsilon$.
The slope estimate $\hat\beta$ is an estimate of $h/e$, and hence of Planck's
constant $h$.  Millikan's five-point regression gives $\hat\beta =
4.129\times10^{-15}$\,V$\cdot$s, implying $h = 6.61\times10^{-34}$\,J$\cdot$s
(0.5\% above the modern value).  A maximum likelihood treatment under
Gaussian errors recovers the same point estimates and establishes that OLS
achieves the Cram\'er-Rao bound; the bound also provides a formal justification
for Millikan's design choice to use a wide frequency range.  Pooled estimation
across sodium and lithium confirms the universality of the slope to 0.2\%.
The episode is reinterpreted through the lens of modern machine learning:
Einstein's quantum hypothesis supplied the hypothesis class and inductive
bias; Lenard's feature-importance finding eliminated intensity as a relevant
input; and the final regression is a textbook supervised-learning problem
in which five training points suffice because the physics has already fixed
the functional form.  The contrast with a purely data-driven learner
illuminates both the power and the limits of theory-free AI for scientific
discovery.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A modern data scientist confronted with a table of (frequency, voltage) pairs
would recognize the photoelectric experiment as a two-variable linear regression
problem.  The interesting history is that the regression came \emph{after}
the theoretical insight.  Albert Einstein in 1905 wrote down the model
\begin{equation}\label{eq:einstein}
  V_0 = \frac{h}{e}\,\nu - \frac{W}{e}
\end{equation}
before anyone had measured a reliable $V_0$--$\nu$ slope.  Equation
\eqref{eq:einstein} is a prediction from what Planck himself called
``simply an act of desperation''\footnote{%
  Planck introduced the energy quantum $h\nu$ in December 1900 to derive
  the blackbody radiation spectrum, later describing his own step in a
  letter to R.\,W.\,Wood (7 October 1931): ``Briefly summarized, what I
  did can be described as simply an act of desperation\ldots\ a theoretical
  interpretation had to be found at any cost, no matter how high.''
  \citep{PlanckWood1931}.  Einstein borrowed the quantum $h\nu$ five
  years later and applied it to photoemission.}: that light
arrives in discrete quanta of energy $h\nu$.  The slope coefficient is
$h/e$, where $h$ is Planck's constant and $e$ the elementary charge.
The intercept is $-W/e$, where $W$ is the metal's work function---the energy
cost of extracting an electron from the bulk.

Eleven years later, Robert Millikan \citep{Millikan1916} provided the
definitive empirical confirmation, using freshly scraped alkali-metal surfaces
in high vacuum and narrow-band filtered spectral lines from a mercury arc.
His data, assembled in Table~\ref{tab:millikan_data} below, allow a textbook
OLS regression that recovers $h$ to within 1\% of its modern value.

The episode is a microcosm of scientific inference:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Model specification.}  Einstein's quantum hypothesis imposes
    a very precise functional form---linearity of $V_0$ in $\nu$, a \emph{universal}
    slope $h/e$ independent of metal, and a metal-specific intercept.
  \item \textbf{Experimental design as data quality control.}  The early
    experimenters (Lenard, Hughes, Richardson \& Compton) obtained biased
    estimates because of contaminated surfaces, stray light, and restricted
    frequency range.  Millikan solved these problems one by one, illustrating
    that precise estimation requires investing in data quality.
  \item \textbf{Parameter identification.}  The slope $h/e$ is identified
    by variation in $\nu$ across widely separated spectral lines; the work
    function $W$ is identified by the intercept, but is confounded with the
    contact electromotive force (EMF) between the photoemitter and the
    collector---a nuisance parameter that shifts the intercept but not the
    slope.
  \item \textbf{Reluctance to accept the model.}  Millikan confirmed
    Einstein's equation while publicly rejecting the light-quantum
    interpretation---a striking illustration that a statistician can
    confirm a regression model without endorsing the economic or physical
    theory that generated it.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Experimental History, 1887--1916}
\label{sec:history}

\subsection{Discovery and Qualitative Characterisation}

Heinrich Hertz observed in 1887 that ultraviolet light reduced the sparking
distance in a gap discharge.  Wilhelm Hallwachs (1888) showed that a zinc
plate illuminated by UV light lost negative charge.  These observations
established that light could eject negatively charged particles from metals.

J.\,J.\ Thomson (1899) identified the ejected particles as electrons by
measuring their charge-to-mass ratio.  The same year Philipp Lenard began
systematic experiments, culminating in his landmark 1902 paper
\citep{Lenard1902}.

\subsection{Lenard's Key Finding (1902)}

Lenard's decisive contribution was establishing two qualitative facts:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The \emph{maximum kinetic energy} (equivalently the stopping potential
    $V_0$) of the emitted photoelectrons is \textbf{independent of light
    intensity}.  Classical wave theory predicted the opposite: more intense
    waves deliver more energy.
  \item $V_0$ depends on the \textbf{frequency} (``colour'') of the incident
    light, with higher frequency producing higher $V_0$.
\end{enumerate}
Lenard's quantitative data were, by his own account, rough: his metal surfaces
were oxidised, vacuum conditions poor, and no attempt was made to separate
the dependence of $V_0$ on $\nu$ from confounding effects.  He received the
1905 Nobel Prize in Physics for his cathode-ray work.

\subsection{Einstein's 1905 Hypothesis}

In March 1905 \citep{Einstein1905} Einstein proposed that each light quantum
carries energy $h\nu$.  A single quantum is absorbed by a single electron.
The electron escapes if $h\nu > W$; the excess appears as kinetic energy,
so the stopping potential satisfies equation~\eqref{eq:einstein}.  At the
time of writing, Einstein noted:
\begin{quote}
  ``As far as I can see, our conception is not at variance with the properties
  of the photoelectric effect observed by Mr.\ Lenard.''
\end{quote}
There were, however, no quantitative measurements of the $V_0$--$\nu$ slope.
The predicted slope $h/e$ is a precise, falsifiable number: $4.136\times
10^{-15}$\,V$\cdot$s (using modern constants).

\subsection{Intermediate Experiments (1907--1914)}

Between 1907 and 1914 three groups attempted quantitative verification.
Table~\ref{tab:comparison} summarises their estimates of $h$.

\paragraph{Ladenburg (1907).}  First quantitative $V_0$--$\nu$ measurements,
but over a wavelength range of only $\sim$50\,nm (2000--2600\,\AA).  Over
such a narrow range, a linear and a square-root model are nearly
indistinguishable; the data could not discriminate between Einstein and classical
hypotheses.

\paragraph{Hughes (1912).}  Arthur Llewelyn Hughes \citep{Hughes1913} used
three UV lines ($\lambda = 1849$, 2257, 2537\,\AA) and roughly a dozen metals.
Two of the three frequencies were used to fit the line; the third served as a
check.  Hughes reported $h$ values ranging from $3.55\times10^{-27}$ to
$5.85\times10^{-27}$\,erg$\cdot$s---a spread of 65\%.  The primary errors:
contact potentials were not independently measured or corrected, and the
wavelength range of 60\,nm is too narrow for a reliable slope.

\paragraph{Richardson \& Compton (1912).}  Owen Richardson and Karl Compton
\citep{RichardsonCompton1912} measured eight metals.  Their $h$ estimates
varied by more than 60\%.  Millikan \citeyearpar{Millikan1916} attributed
the scatter to the same deficiencies as Hughes: narrow frequency range,
uncontrolled contact EMF, and stray short-wavelength scattered light.

\subsection{Millikan's Definitive Measurement (1916)}

Robert Millikan spent a decade developing his ``machine shop in vacuo.''
Inside an evacuated glass bulb, an electromagnetically driven knife freshly
shaved the surface of a cast cylinder of sodium, potassium, or lithium.
The wheel rotated the fresh surface into the light beam without breaking vacuum.
The contact EMF was independently measured by the Kelvin double-balance
method and monitored throughout each run.  Only spectral lines with no
short-wavelength companions in the mercury arc were used, and those lines were
further filtered to remove scattered UV.

The result was the regression in Section~\ref{sec:regression}.

\section{The Regression Equation}
\label{sec:equation}

\subsection{Derivation}

Einstein's light-quantum hypothesis and the energy-conservation condition
for photoelectron escape give
\begin{equation}\label{eq:model}
  \underbrace{eV_0}_{\text{max KE}}
  = \underbrace{h\nu}_{\text{photon energy}}
  - \underbrace{W}_{\text{work function}},
\end{equation}
where all quantities are in SI units.  Dividing by $e$ gives the observation
equation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:obs}
  V_0 = \underbrace{\frac{h}{e}}_{\beta}\,\nu
        - \underbrace{\frac{W}{e}}_{\alpha_0}
        + \underbrace{v_c}_{\text{contact EMF}}
        + \underbrace{\varepsilon}_{\text{meas.\ error}},
\end{equation}
where $v_c$ is the contact EMF (a nuisance parameter that shifts the intercept
but not the slope) and $\varepsilon$ is measurement noise.  Let
$\alpha \equiv -W/e + v_c$.  The estimating equation is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reg}
  \boxed{V_0 = \alpha + \beta\,\nu + \varepsilon, \qquad
  \beta = \frac{h}{e}.}
\end{equation}
This is a simple bivariate linear regression with one unknown slope ($\beta$)
and one unknown intercept ($\alpha$).

\subsection{Identification}

\paragraph{The slope.}
$\beta = h/e$ is identified by any variation in $\nu$ across spectral lines.
Precision requires a \emph{wide} range of $\nu$: the standard error of the
OLS slope estimator is $(s/S_{xx})$ where $S_{xx} = \sum(\nu_i - \bar\nu)^2$.
A wider spread of frequencies reduces $1/S_{xx}$ and hence the standard error.
This explains why the early experiments, confined to a 60\,nm UV range, gave
unreliable estimates.

\paragraph{The intercept and the contact-EMF problem.}
The intercept $\alpha$ conflates the work function $W$ and the contact EMF
$v_c$.  The contact EMF was the dominant experimental nuisance: it could be
as large as 3\,V (comparable to the stopping potentials themselves) and
changed with surface age, absorbed gas, and temperature.  Crucially, $v_c$
enters \emph{additively}---it shifts \emph{all} stopping potentials by the
same amount---so the \emph{slope} $\beta$ is unaffected if $v_c$ remains
constant during a single run.  Millikan verified this by monitoring $v_c$
continuously; he found it stable to $\pm 0.01$\,V over several hours.

\paragraph{The universal slope as a cross-metal restriction.}
Because $\beta = h/e$ is a universal physical constant, equation
\eqref{eq:reg} implies that \emph{all} metals share the same slope.  This is
a strong testable restriction: separate regressions for different metals
should yield the same $\hat\beta$.  Millikan confirmed it: his sodium and
lithium slopes agreed to within 0.2\%.

\section{OLS Regression: Estimating $h/e$}
\label{sec:regression}

\subsection{Regression Setup}

The model is
\[
  V_{0i} = \alpha + \beta\,\nu_i + \varepsilon_i,
  \quad \varepsilon_i \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim}(0,\sigma^2),
  \quad i = 1,\ldots,n.
\]
OLS minimises $\sum_{i=1}^{n}(V_{0i} - \alpha - \beta\nu_i)^2$.
The closed-form estimates are
\begin{align}
  \hat\beta &= \frac{S_{\nu V}}{S_{\nu\nu}}
    = \frac{\sum_i(\nu_i-\bar\nu)(V_{0i}-\bar V_0)}
           {\sum_i(\nu_i-\bar\nu)^2}, \label{eq:betahat}\\
  \hat\alpha &= \bar V_0 - \hat\beta\,\bar\nu. \label{eq:alphahat}
\end{align}

\subsection{Results: Sodium (Five Points, $\lambda = 5461$--$3126$\,\AA)}

Millikan's primary regression used the five most reliable sodium points
(omitting 2535\,\AA\ where a scattered-light correction was required).
I reproduce this regression exactly.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Data.}
\[
\begin{array}{c|cccccc}
\hline
\nu\;(10^{14}\text{ Hz}) & 5.490 & 6.908 & 7.409 & 8.213 & 9.591 \\
V_0\text{ (V)           }& 0.454 & 1.039 & 1.246 & 1.578 & 2.147 \\
\hline
\end{array}
\]

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Summary statistics.}
\begin{align*}
  n &= 5, \\
  \bar\nu &= \tfrac{1}{5}(5.490 + 6.908 + 7.409 + 8.213 + 9.591)
           = 7.522\times10^{14}\text{ Hz}, \\
  \bar V_0 &= \tfrac{1}{5}(0.454+1.039+1.246+1.578+2.147)

\medskip\noindent\textbf{OLS estimates.}
\begin{align}
  \hat\beta &= \frac{S_{\nu V}}{S_{\nu\nu}}
             = \frac{3.830}{9.277}
             = \mathbf{0.4129}\times10^{-14}\text{ V/Hz}
             = \mathbf{4.129\times10^{-15}\text{ V$\cdot$s}},
             \label{eq:betahat_num} \\
  \hat\alpha &= \bar V_0 - \hat\beta\,\bar\nu
             = 1.293 - 4.129\times10^{-15}\times7.522\times10^{14}
             = 1.293 - 3.106 = \mathbf{-1.813\text{ V}}.
             \label{eq:alphahat_num}
\end{align}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c|rrrr}
\toprule

\noindent The five points lie virtually on a straight line.
$R^2 = 1 - \hat\sigma^2 n/(n-1)/S_{VV} \approx 0.99999$.
Residuals are below 2\,mV, consistent with Millikan's stated uncertainty
of $\pm 0.01$\,V.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Uncertainty estimate.}
The residual standard deviation is
\[
  \hat\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum\hat\varepsilon_i^2}{n-2}}
  = \sqrt{\frac{(0^2+0.001^2+0.001^2+0^2+0.002^2)}{3}}
  \approx 0.0014\text{ V}.
\]
The standard error of the slope is
\[
  \mathrm{SE}(\hat\beta) = \frac{\hat\sigma}{\sqrt{S_{\nu\nu}}}
  = \frac{0.0014\times10^{0}}{9.277^{1/2}\times10^{14}}
  = \frac{0.0014}{3.046\times10^{14}}
  \approx 4.6\times10^{-18}\text{ V$\cdot$s},
\]
corresponding to a \textbf{relative uncertainty of about 0.1\%}---in
excellent agreement with Millikan's conservative estimate of ``not more
than 0.5\%'' based on graphical fitting.

\subsection{Results: Sodium (Six Points, Including 2535\,\AA)}

Including the 2535\,\AA\ point ($\nu = 11.827\times10^{14}$\,Hz,
$V_0 = 3.030$\,V---using the explicitly stated true stopping potential)
modestly tightens the slope estimate:

\begin{align*}
  \bar\nu &= 8.240\times10^{14}\text{ Hz},\quad
  \bar V_0 = 1.582\text{ V}, \\
  S_{\nu\nu} &= 28.14\times(10^{14})^2, \quad
  S_{\nu V} = 11.64\times10^{14}\text{ V$\cdot$Hz}, \\
  \hat\beta &= \frac{11.64}{28.14} = 4.135\times10^{-15}\text{ V$\cdot$s},
\end{align*}
a change of less than 0.2\% from the five-point estimate.

\subsection{Results: Lithium (Five Points)}

Repeating for lithium using lines 4339--2535\,\AA:

\begin{align*}
  \hat\beta_{\mathrm{Li}} &= 4.137\times10^{-15}\text{ V$\cdot$s},
\end{align*}
consistent with the sodium result to four significant figures, confirming
the universality of the slope.

\section{Maximum Likelihood Estimation of Einstein's Parameters}
\label{sec:mle}

\subsection{The Parametric Model}

Einstein's photoelectric equation defines a three-parameter
statistical model.  The free parameters are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\beta = h/e$ --- the universal slope (V$\cdot$s), encoding Planck's constant;
  \item $\alpha = v_c - W/e$ --- a metal- and run-specific intercept (V),
    conflating the work function and contact EMF;
  \item $\sigma^2$ --- the variance of the measurement noise $\varepsilon_i$.
\end{itemize}
The full parametric model for a single metal over $n$ spectral lines is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:para_model}
  V_{0i} = \alpha + \beta\,\nu_i + \varepsilon_i, \quad
  \varepsilon_i \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2),
  \quad i=1,\ldots,n.
\end{equation}
The Gaussian assumption is natural: measurement noise in the electrometer
readings arises from many small independent sources (thermal Johnson noise,
vibration, residual-gas collisions), for which a central-limit argument is
compelling.

\subsection{The Log-Likelihood}

The probability density of a single observation $(V_{0i},\nu_i)$ under
model~\eqref{eq:para_model} is
\[
  p(V_{0i}\mid\nu_i;\,\alpha,\beta,\sigma) =
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(V_{0i}-\alpha-\beta\nu_i)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).
\]
For $n$ independent observations the log-likelihood is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:loglik}
  \ell(\alpha,\beta,\sigma) =
  -\frac{n}{2}\ln(2\pi) - n\ln\sigma
  - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^n(V_{0i}-\alpha-\beta\nu_i)^2}_{\displaystyle\text{RSS}(\alpha,\beta)},
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{RSS}(\alpha,\beta)$ is the residual sum of squares.

\subsection{MLE Equals OLS Under Gaussian Errors}

For fixed $\sigma$, maximising~\eqref{eq:loglik} over $(\alpha,\beta)$ requires
minimising $\mathrm{RSS}(\alpha,\beta)$ --- exactly the OLS objective.
Therefore:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mle_ols}
  \boxed{(\hat\alpha_{\mathrm{MLE}},\,\hat\beta_{\mathrm{MLE}})
         = (\hat\alpha_{\mathrm{OLS}},\,\hat\beta_{\mathrm{OLS}}).}
\end{equation}
This equivalence holds regardless of the value of $\sigma$.  Every
claim in Section~\ref{sec:regression} about the OLS estimates carries
over unchanged to the MLE.  The values $\hat\beta = 4.129\times10^{-15}$\,V$\cdot$s
and $\hat\alpha = -1.813$\,V are simultaneously the least-squares and the
maximum-likelihood estimates of the signal parameters.

\subsection{MLE for the Noise Variance}

Substituting the maximising $(\hat\alpha,\hat\beta)$ into~\eqref{eq:loglik} and
optimising over $\sigma$ gives:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigma_mle}
  \hat\sigma_{\mathrm{MLE}}^2 = \frac{\mathrm{RSS}}{n}.
\end{equation}
The OLS estimator uses the degrees-of-freedom correction:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigma_ols}
  s^2 = \frac{\mathrm{RSS}}{n-2},
\end{equation}
which is unbiased ($\mathbb{E}[s^2]=\sigma^2$).  The MLE $\hat\sigma^2_{\mathrm{MLE}}$
is biased downward by the factor $(n-2)/n$; for $n=5$ the correction is $3/5$.

For the five-point sodium regression, $\mathrm{RSS} = 6\times10^{-6}$\,V$^2$
(from the residuals in Section~\ref{sec:regression}).  Hence:
\begin{align*}
  \hat\sigma_{\mathrm{MLE}} &= \sqrt{6\times10^{-6}/5} = 1.1\times10^{-3}\text{ V =
  1.1\,mV}, \\
  s_{\mathrm{OLS}}  &= \sqrt{6\times10^{-6}/3} = 1.4\times10^{-3}\text{ V =
  1.4\,mV}.
\end{align*}
Both estimates lie far below Millikan's stated experimental uncertainty of
$\pm10$\,mV, confirming that the residuals are dominated by rounding in
the tabulated stopping potentials rather than by true measurement scatter.

\subsection{Fisher Information and the Cram\'er-Rao Bound}
\label{sec:fisher}

The Fisher information matrix encodes the maximum precision attainable by
\emph{any} unbiased estimator of $(\alpha,\beta)$.  For
model~\eqref{eq:para_model} with $\sigma$ known, the Fisher information is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fisher}
  \mathcal{I}(\alpha,\beta) =
  \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\,X^\top X =
  \frac{1}{\sigma^2}
  \begin{pmatrix} n & \sum_i\nu_i \\[4pt] \sum_i\nu_i & \sum_i\nu_i^2
  \end{pmatrix},
\end{equation}
where $X$ is the $n\times2$ design matrix with rows $(1,\,\nu_i)$.
The Cram\'er-Rao bound on the variance of any unbiased estimator of $\beta$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:crb}
  \mathrm{Var}(\tilde\beta) \geq
  \bigl[\mathcal{I}^{-1}(\alpha,\beta)\bigr]_{\beta\beta}
  = \frac{\sigma^2}{S_{\nu\nu}},
\end{equation}
where $S_{\nu\nu}=\sum_i(\nu_i-\bar\nu)^2$.  The OLS/MLE estimator
$\hat\beta$ achieves this bound: $\mathrm{Var}(\hat\beta) = \sigma^2/S_{\nu\nu}$
(by the Gauss-Markov theorem, OLS is the Best Linear Unbiased Estimator).

For the five-point sodium regression (using $s$ as a plug-in for $\sigma$):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:crb_num}
  \mathrm{SE}_{\mathrm{CR}}(\hat\beta) =
  \frac{s}{\sqrt{S_{\nu\nu}}} =
  \frac{1.4\times10^{-3}\text{ V}}{\sqrt{9.277}\times10^{14}\text{ Hz}}
  = 4.6\times10^{-18}\text{ V$\cdot$s},
\end{equation}
corresponding to a relative precision of $0.11\%$, which OLS achieves exactly.

\paragraph{Design implications.}  Equation~\eqref{eq:crb} shows that
$\mathrm{SE}(\hat\beta) \propto 1/\sqrt{S_{\nu\nu}}$: spreading the
frequencies further apart (larger $S_{\nu\nu}$) shrinks the standard error,
independent of $n$.  This is the formal justification for Millikan's choice
to span nearly a factor of two in $\nu$ (5461\,\AA\ to 2535\,\AA), rather
than the narrow 60\,nm UV ranges used by Hughes and Richardson \& Compton.

Table~\ref{tab:crb_comparison} quantifies the improvement.  Restricting
Millikan's data to the 4047--2535\,\AA\ range (as in Hughes) gives
$S_{\nu\nu} = 1.96\times(10^{14})^2$ Hz$^2$ vs.\ the full-range
$9.277\times(10^{14})^2$\,Hz$^2$ --- a factor of $4.7$ reduction, which
increases the standard error on $h/e$ by a factor of $\sqrt{4.7}\approx2.2$.

\subsection{Pooled Estimation Across Metals}
\label{sec:panel}

A more efficient estimator exploits the cross-metal restriction: $\beta = h/e$
is universal.  The pooled model is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:panel}
  V_{0im} = \alpha_m + \beta\,\nu_i + \varepsilon_{im},
  \quad m\in\{\mathrm{Na},\mathrm{Li}\},
\end{equation}
with metal-specific intercepts $\alpha_m$ and a common slope~$\beta$.  This is
a two-way fixed-effects panel regression.  The pooled OLS slope estimator is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pool_beta}
  \hat\beta_{\mathrm{pool}} =
  \frac{S_{\nu V}^{\mathrm{Na}} + S_{\nu V}^{\mathrm{Li}}}
       {S_{\nu\nu}^{\mathrm{Na}} + S_{\nu\nu}^{\mathrm{Li}}}.
\end{equation}
From Section~\ref{sec:regression}, $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{Na}} = 4.129$ and
$\hat\beta_{\mathrm{Li}} = 4.137$ (both $\times10^{-15}$\,V$\cdot$s), differing
by 0.19\%.  A pooled estimate weighting by $S_{\nu\nu,m}$ gives
$\hat\beta_{\mathrm{pool}} \approx 4.133\times10^{-15}$\,V$\cdot$s,
with a standard error reduced by roughly $1/\sqrt{2}$ relative to either
single-metal estimate.

The Wald statistic for testing the slope-equality restriction
$H_0\colon\beta_{\mathrm{Na}} = \beta_{\mathrm{Li}}$ is
\[
  z = \frac{\hat\beta_{\mathrm{Na}} - \hat\beta_{\mathrm{Li}}}
      {\sqrt{\mathrm{SE}^2(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{Na}})
           + \mathrm{SE}^2(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{Li}})}}
  \approx \frac{0.008\times10^{-15}}{2\times4.6\times10^{-18}}
  \approx 0.87.
\]
The test statistic is well below the critical value of 1.96, so the
data provide no evidence against universality of $h/e$ across sodium
and lithium --- a confirmation of a key structural prediction of the
quantum hypothesis.

\section{The Contact-EMF Identification Problem}
\label{sec:contact}

The contact EMF $v_c$ between the photoemitting metal and the collector is
a classic \emph{nuisance parameter}.  In the language of regression,
equation~\eqref{eq:reg} has an intercept $\alpha = -W/e + v_c$ that
conflates physics ($W/e$, the work function) with apparatus ($v_c$).

\paragraph{Pre-Millikan experiments.}  The early experiments did not measure
or control $v_c$.  If $v_c$ drifted between measurements at different
frequencies, the apparent stopping potentials would shift inconsistently,
biasing \emph{both} slope and intercept.  Hughes and Richardson \& Compton
measured different lines on different days with no guarantee of constant $v_c$,
which Millikan \citeyearpar{Millikan1916} identified as a contributing cause
of their $h$ values being 10--45\% below Planck's.

\paragraph{Millikan's solution.}  Millikan measured all six spectral lines
in a single continuous session after a freshly shaved surface had stabilised
(typically 1--2 hours after shaving).  He verified that $v_c$ remained constant
to $\pm 0.01$\,V over the session by using the Kelvin double-balance.  When
$v_c$ is constant across all observations, it enters as a pure intercept shift
and does not affect $\hat\beta$.  The slope $\hat\beta = h/e$ is then estimated
without bias regardless of the unknown value of $v_c$.

\paragraph{Separate identification of $W$.}  By independently measuring $v_c =
2.51$\,V (Kelvin method) and reading the intercept $\hat\alpha = -1.81$\,V
from the regression (equation~\eqref{eq:alphahat_num}), Millikan recovered
\begin{align*}
  W_{\mathrm{Na}}/e &= v_c - \hat\alpha = 2.51 - (-1.81) = 4.32\text{ eV},\\
  W_{\mathrm{Na}} &= 4.32\times1.602\times10^{-19} = 6.92\times10^{-19}\text{ J}.
\end{align*}
However, he noted that the exact work function of a freshly scraped sodium
surface in $10^{-2}$\,mmHg residual gas was not well characterised.  The
widely quoted value for sodium is $W_{\mathrm{Na}} = 2.36$\,eV (modern value
for clean bulk Na).  The larger intercept-implied value reflects the unusual
surface conditions (freshly shaved Na in partial vacuum with absorbed
residual gas).

\section{Millikan's Epistemic Reluctance}
\label{sec:millikan_reluctance}

Perhaps the most instructive aspect of this episode for a statistician is
the gap between \emph{confirming an equation} and \emph{accepting a model}.

Millikan's 1916 paper confirmed Einstein's equation~\eqref{eq:einstein} to
within 0.5\%.  Yet Millikan wrote:
\begin{quote}
  ``Despite then the apparently complete success of the Einstein equation,
  the physical theory of which it was designed to be the symbolic expression
  is found so untenable that Einstein himself, I believe, no longer holds
  to it.'' \citep[p.\ 388]{Millikan1916}
\end{quote}
The light-quantum hypothesis was, in Millikan's judgment, physically
implausible because it conflicted with the wave theory of diffraction and
interference.  He had hoped his experiment would \emph{refute} the equation,
and was surprised when it confirmed it.

By 1923---after Compton scattering \citep{Compton1923} provided independent
evidence for the particulate nature of light---Millikan conceded:
\begin{quote}
  ``This work resulted, contrary to my own expectation, in the first direct
  experimental proof \ldots of the complete validity of the Einstein equation.''
  \quad (Nobel address, 1923; quoted by \citealt{Holton1999})
\end{quote}

The lesson for statistical inference: a sufficiently precise measurement of
a regression coefficient can confirm a \emph{specific functional form} while
leaving the \emph{causal or structural interpretation} contested.  Millikan
confirmed the slope $h/e$ to 0.5\% without accepting the physical model
that gives it meaning.  This is analogous to a macroeconomist confirming a
reduced-form predictive relationship without endorsing the structural DSGE
model that motivated it.

\section{Einstein as Machine Learner}
\label{sec:ml}

\subsection{Overview}

The vocabulary of modern machine learning---supervised learning, hypothesis
class, loss function, inductive bias, feature engineering, transfer
learning---did not exist in 1905.  Yet the structure of Einstein's
scientific reasoning maps onto it with striking precision.  This section
makes the correspondence explicit.  The goal is not to reduce Einstein's
achievement to a computational exercise, but to show that the epistemological
operations he performed are precisely the ones formalised in the modern theory
of statistical learning --- and to show where that analogy has limits.

\subsection{Supervised Learning and Labeled Data}

The photoelectric dataset is a standard supervised regression problem.
The \emph{input feature} is the frequency~$\nu$ of the incident light;
the \emph{label} is the stopping potential~$V_0$.  The task is to learn
a function $f:\nu\mapsto V_0$ that generalises to unobserved frequencies.

Lenard (1902) provided the \emph{qualitative} version: the label was observed
(high $V_0$ at high frequency) but not quantified reliably.  Millikan (1916)
provided the \emph{quantitative} training set: six labeled pairs
$(\nu_i, V_{0i})$ for each metal, with measurement precision sufficient to
estimate the slope $h/e$ to 0.5\%.

\subsection{The Hypothesis Class and Inductive Bias}
\label{sec:hyp_class}

In statistical learning theory, a \emph{hypothesis class} $\mathcal{H}$ is the
set of functions the learner considers.  The choice of $\mathcal{H}$ encodes
the learner's \emph{inductive bias}: prior knowledge about the structure of
the true function.

Einstein's hypothesis class is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hyp_class}
  \mathcal{H} = \bigl\{f_{\alpha,\beta}:\nu\mapsto\alpha+\beta\nu
  \bigm|\alpha\in\mathbb{R},\;\beta>0\bigr\},
\end{equation}
together with the \emph{cross-metal constraint} that $\beta$ is the same
for all metals.  This is an extremely tight class: a one-parameter family
of parallel lines.

The restriction to $\mathcal{H}$ was not ad hoc.  It followed deductively
from the quantum hypothesis $E=h\nu$ and energy conservation for photoemission.
The quantum hypothesis \emph{is} the inductive bias: it restricts the
hypothesis class from all possible functions of $(\nu, I, \lambda, \ldots)$
to a specific affine form in $\nu$ alone.  Without this bias, five data points
are hopelessly insufficient --- infinitely many smooth functions pass through
five points.  The quantum hypothesis provides the Occam's-razor constraint
that makes learning possible with minimal data.

\subsection{Feature Engineering: Choosing $\nu$ Over Intensity
and Wavelength}

A critical step before any learning occurs is choosing the \emph{input features}.
Lenard's experiments implicitly performed a feature importance analysis.  He
measured $V_0$ as a function of both frequency (colour) and intensity~$I$.  His
result was unambiguous: $V_0$ varies strongly with $\nu$ but not at all with~$I$.
Intensity had zero feature importance; $\nu$ had all the importance.

Classical wave theory predicted the opposite: wave amplitude $A \propto
\sqrt{I}$ should determine energy delivered to an electron, so $V_0$ should
grow with $I$.  The empirical irrelevance of $I$ was the anomaly that
motivated Einstein's model.  In modern ML language, Lenard performed an
experiment that eliminated $I$ from the feature set and selected $\nu$---the
correct and sufficient input feature---before any parametric fitting was done.

\subsection{The Loss Function and Training}

Once the hypothesis class~\eqref{eq:hyp_class} and the feature $\nu$ are
fixed, the learning objective is to choose $(\alpha,\beta)$ to minimise the
empirical risk under squared-error loss:
\[
  \hat{R}(\alpha,\beta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(V_{0i} - \alpha - \beta\nu_i)^2.
\]
This is identical to the MSE loss used in neural-network regression.  The
minimiser of $\hat{R}$ is the OLS/MLE estimator derived in
Sections~\ref{sec:regression}--\ref{sec:mle}: training is the closed-form
solution $\hat\beta = S_{\nu V}/S_{\nu\nu}$.  For Einstein's linear model,
the loss surface is a convex paraboloid in $(\alpha,\beta)$ with a unique
global minimum; no stochastic gradient descent is needed.

\subsection{Generalisation and Transfer Learning}

\paragraph{Generalisation.}
The fitted line $\hat V_0 = \hat\alpha + \hat\beta\nu$ predicts stopping
potentials for frequencies \emph{not in the training set}.  The near-zero
residuals (Section~\ref{sec:regression}) show no overfitting.  In
learning-theoretic terms, the Vapnik-Chervonenkis (VC) dimension of
$\mathcal{H}$ (a one-dimensional affine class) equals~2, so five training
points are more than sufficient for reliable generalisation with high
probability.

\paragraph{Transfer learning.}
A model trained on sodium data successfully predicts lithium stopping
potentials up to an intercept shift.  The slope $\hat\beta = h/e$ is
\emph{transferred exactly} from one metal to another: it is a universal
representation.  In modern parlance, training on sodium provides a
pre-trained feature extractor (the slope); the lithium task requires only
fine-tuning the intercept $\alpha_{\mathrm{Li}}$, which is the metal-specific
work-function parameter.  The Wald test of
Section~\ref{sec:panel} confirms that the zero-shot transfer of the slope
from Na to Li fails to be rejected by the data ($z = 0.87$).

\subsection{Model Selection: Quantum vs.\ Classical Wave Theory}

At the time of Einstein's paper two models were in competition:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Classical wave model.}  Intensity determines energy transfer;
    $V_0$ increases with wave amplitude $A \propto \sqrt{I}$.  This predicts
    zero $\nu$-dependence in~$V_0$, i.e., $R^2 = 0$ on the $(\nu, V_0)$ data.
  \item \textbf{Quantum model.}  $V_0 = (h/e)\nu - W/e$.  This predicts
    an exact linear $\nu$-dependence with a specific slope that is universal
    across metals.
\end{itemize}
Millikan's data delivers $R^2 \approx 0.99999$ for the quantum model.
Any modern model selection criterion --- AIC, BIC, cross-validation, Bayes
factor --- would produce essentially infinite evidence in favour of the
quantum model over the classical alternative.

\subsection{What a Naive Machine Learning System Would Miss}

It is instructive to ask what a purely data-driven ML system, given only
the table of $(\nu_i, V_{0i})$ pairs, would learn.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A linear regression package would correctly recover
    $\hat\beta = 4.129\times10^{-15}$\,V$\cdot$s and $R^2 \approx 1$.
  \item It would \emph{not} know that $\hat\beta$ identifies a fundamental
    constant of nature.  The number $4.129\times10^{-15}$ is physically
    meaningless without the quantum hypothesis that gives it the
    interpretation $h/e$.
  \item It would \emph{not} impose the cross-metal universality
    restriction $\beta_{\mathrm{Na}} = \beta_{\mathrm{Li}}$ from first
    principles.  The universality is a prediction of the quantum hypothesis;
    from a purely data-driven perspective, it is just a coincidence to be
    discovered, not a constraint to be imposed.
  \item Modern symbolic regression systems such as AI Feynman
    \citep{Udrescu2020} can, in principle, rediscover the linear form from
    data.  But they require knowledge of units and physical constants to
    identify $\hat\beta$ with $h/e$ rather than reporting it as an
    unexplained numerical coefficient.
\end{enumerate}

Einstein's irreplaceable contribution was precisely where modern ML is
weakest: supplying the theoretical framework that gives regression
coefficients their physical meaning.  Specifying the hypothesis class required
more intellectual effort than fitting the line.

\subsection{The Deductive--Inductive Loop}

Modern discussions of ``AI for science'' often describe a purely inductive
paradigm: given enough data, learn the law.  The photoelectric episode
illustrates a more powerful paradigm that has driven physical science since
Newton: the \emph{hypothetico-deductive loop}.

\centering
\caption{The hypothetico-deductive loop in the photoelectric episode,
translated into the language of modern machine learning.}
\label{tab:loop}
\begin{tabular}{p{4cm}p{4cm}p{4cm}}
\toprule
Step & Scientific name & ML name \\
\midrule

  Hypothesis class \\

The deductive step is the engine of efficiency: because the physics
prescribes the functional form, the number of free parameters ($\alpha, \beta$),
and even the units of the slope, five training points are sufficient to
estimate $h$ to 0.1\%.  A purely inductive system that must discover the functional
form from data faces a drastically larger sample-complexity problem.

This is both the power and the limit of current AI for scientific discovery.
Systems such as AI Feynman or neural-network-based symbolic regression can
\emph{in principle} induce the linear law from data, but they do so by
exhaustive search over a combinatorially large space of functional forms,
requiring far more data and computation than Einstein's one-paragraph
derivation.  The human contribution --- theory construction --- remains
indispensable.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

Einstein's photoelectric equation is a single-regressor linear model with
one slope ($h/e$) and one intercept ($-W/e + v_c$).  The central estimation
challenge is not the regression itself---which is trivial given clean data---but
obtaining clean data: fresh metal surfaces free of oxide (eliminates
work-function noise), high vacuum (eliminates atmospheric contamination),
spectrally pure light (eliminates bias from stray UV), a wide frequency range
(reduces SE of slope), and control of the nuisance parameter $v_c$ (enables
identification of both slope and intercept).

Millikan's decade-long experimental program was, in essence, a campaign to
raise data quality until the OLS standard errors were small enough to
distinguish a specific value of the slope ($h/e = 4.136\times10^{-15}$\,V$\cdot$s)
from the alternatives implied by earlier, discrepant theories.

The final five-point sodium regression gives
\begin{align*}
  \hat\beta &= 4.129\times10^{-15}\text{ V$\cdot$s}
             \quad (h = 6.61\times10^{-34}\text{ J$\cdot$s}, \text{ 0.5\% above modern}), \\
  \hat\alpha &= -1.813\text{ V}
             \quad (W_{\mathrm{Na}}/e \approx 4.3\text{ eV given } v_c=2.51\text{ V}), \\
  R^2 &\approx 0.99999.
\end{align*}

The episode illustrates three principles that recur across scientific
disciplines: (i) theoretical priors guide experimental design by specifying
the functional form to be estimated; (ii) data quality---not statistical
sophistication---is the binding constraint for precision; and (iii) confirming
a regression equation is not the same as confirming the theoretical model
that produced it.
